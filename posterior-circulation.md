## Vertebral Arteries

### Normal Anatomy
The vertebral arteries typically arise from the subclavian arteries, ascend through the transverse foramina of C6-C1, enter the skull through the foramen magnum, and unite to form the basilar artery.

### Variations

#### Origin Variations (Right Vertebral Artery)
The right vertebral artery may arise from:
- First part of the subclavian artery (typical)
- Second part of the subclavian artery
- Directly from the aortic arch
- Common carotid artery
- External carotid artery

#### Origin Variations (Left Vertebral Artery)
- May arise directly from the aortic arch (relatively common variation)
- Between the left common carotid and left subclavian

#### Course Variations
- May enter transverse foramina at levels other than C6
- May follow anomalous course through vertebral column
- Position and arterial loops more common with advanced age (possibly pathological)
- Retroesophageal course

#### Termination Variations
- May terminate as posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA) without joining contralateral vertebral
- Non-union of vertebral arteries (basilar formed by one vertebral only)
- One vertebral may be rudimentary

#### Asymmetry
- Significant size asymmetry between right and left vertebral arteries is common
- One vessel may be hypoplastic

#### Rare Variations
- In one case, rudimentary vertebral artery stopped short of skull; basilar arose as branch of ICA in neck, entered cranium through hypoglossal canal

## Basilar Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The basilar artery is formed by the union of the two vertebral arteries at the pontomedullary junction. It ascends along the ventral surface of the pons and terminates by dividing into the posterior cerebral arteries.

### Variations

#### Fenestration/Duplication
- May exist as **two longitudinal trunks** united by anastomoses
- May split into two vessels that reunite
- When two vessels fuse, resulting single vessel may have **median sagittal partition**

#### Absence
- Rarely, the basilar artery may be absent
- Blood supply maintained through alternative pathways

#### Course Variations
- May pass through a foramen in the dorsum sellae
- Aberrant branch may arise and pierce dorsum sellae to join internal carotid

#### Origin Variations
- May arise as branch of internal carotid in neck (extremely rare)
- May enter cranial cavity through hypoglossal canal

#### Relationship to Age
Studies suggest atypical artery position and arterial loops may be more common in subjects of advanced age, though this may be pathological rather than congenital.

## Posterior Cerebral Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The posterior cerebral arteries (PCAs) are the terminal branches of the basilar artery, supplying the occipital lobes, inferomedial temporal lobes, and portions of the thalamus.

### Variations

#### Fetal (Embryonic) Origin
- **Incidence: 30%** - One or both PCAs may have an "embryonic origin" from the internal carotid artery
- In these cases, the connection from the basilar artery is hypoplastic
- The posterior communicating artery effectively becomes the proximal PCA

#### Reduplication
- PCA may be duplicated

#### Hypoplastic P-1 Segment
- The P-1 segment (basilar to posterior communicating junction) may be hypoplastic when fetal configuration persists

#### Origin from ICA
- Proximal part of one PCA may be a branch of the internal carotid, connected to the basilar by a very short posterior communicating

### Clinical Significance
Understanding PCA origin is critical because:
- Fetal configuration affects collateral potential
- Relevant for endovascular navigation
- Important for surgical planning

## Posterior Communicating Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The posterior communicating arteries connect the internal carotid arteries to the posterior cerebral arteries, forming the lateral connections between anterior and posterior circulations.

### Variations

#### Absence/Hypoplasia
- May be absent
- May be replaced by a branch
- Frequently hypoplastic (one of the most variable components of Circle of Willis)

#### Fetal Configuration
When the PCA has "fetal origin," the posterior communicating artery is effectively the main supply to the PCA and is typically large.

#### Unusual Connections
- May connect to middle cerebral artery instead of ICA trunk
- Accessory posterior communicating artery may arise from MCA

### Clinical Significance
A hypoplastic or absent posterior communicating artery limits collateral flow between the anterior and posterior circulations, which may be critical during:
- Carotid occlusion
- Basilar artery disease
- Surgical procedures requiring temporary vessel occlusion

### References
Kaplan, H.A. and D.H. Ford. (1966) The Brain Vascular System. Elsevier, Amsterdam.

## Cerebellar Arteries

### Overview
Three paired arteries supply the cerebellum:
- **Superior Cerebellar Artery (SCA)** - from basilar
- **Anterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (AICA)** - from basilar
- **Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (PICA)** - from vertebral

### Anterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (AICA)

#### Absence
- One AICA may be missing

#### Origin Variations
- May arise from PICA

#### Relationship to Abducent Nerve (Sunderland, 1948)
- AICA ran basal to abducent nerve: Right 84%, Left 73%
- AICA ran through abducent nerve: 3 cases on left side
- AICA deeply indented nerve without adverse effect: 16/260 cases

### Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (PICA)

#### Origin Variations

| Origin Location | Incidence |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Intracranial vertebral artery | 61% |
| Lower third of basilar | 52% |
| Middle third of basilar | 46% |
| Upper third of basilar | 2% |

#### Absence
- One PICA may be missing

#### Duplication
- PICA duplicated in 11.1% (Brunner, 1978)

#### Unusual Origins
- May arise from internal carotid artery (rare)
  - Explained by persistence of primitive communicating vessel (presegmental artery)
  - In 5,500 angiographic studies, 11 cerebellar arteries arose directly from ICA
  - Considered persistent primitive trigeminal artery variants
- May arise from posterior meningeal artery
- May provide posterior spinal artery

#### Relationship to Vertebral Artery Termination
- When one vertebral terminates as PICA, it does not contribute to basilar formation

### Embryonic Cerebral Arteries
Four embryonic arteries directly connect the carotid and basilar systems:
1. **Trigeminal** - most frequent persistent type (0.2% in angiograms)
2. **Otic**
3. **Hypoglossal**
4. **Proatlantal**

See [Persistent Embryologic Arteries](persistent-embryologic-arteries.md) for details.
