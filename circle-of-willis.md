## Overview

The Circle of Willis is an anastomotic ring of arterial vessels at the base of the brain connecting the anterior (carotid) and posterior (vertebrobasilar) circulations. This circle is **extremely variable**.

Stehbens regards a normal Circle of Willis to be one in which there is a complete anastomotic polygon, which may show considerable variability not only of its components but also its branches. The so-called normal or textbook description holds true in only about **34.5%** of cases based on a composite review of 1,413 brains. Not even Willis's original illustration conforms to the usual textbook description.

## Components

The complete Circle of Willis consists of:

**Anterior:**
- Anterior cerebral arteries (paired)
- Anterior communicating artery

**Lateral:**
- Internal carotid arteries (paired)

**Posterior:**
- Posterior communicating arteries (paired)
- Posterior cerebral arteries (paired)

## Documented Variations

### Major Variation Studies

| Study | Findings |

### Common Variation Patterns

1. **Hypoplastic segments** - One or more arterial segments reduced in caliber
2. **Absent segments** - Complete absence of one or more components
3. **Duplicated segments** - Particularly the anterior communicating artery
4. **Fetal configuration** - Posterior cerebral artery arising from internal carotid (30% of cases)
5. **Fenestrations** - Splitting and reuniting of arterial segments
6. **Asymmetric caliber** - Significant size differences between paired vessels

### Specific Component Variations

| Component | Variation | Incidence |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Anterior communicating a. | Single | ~81% |
| Anterior communicating a. | Double/triple | ~15% |
| Anterior communicating a. | Absent | 0.22% |
| A-1 segment (ACA) | Hypoplastic | 7% |
| Posterior communicating a. | Hypoplastic/absent | Variable |
| Posterior cerebral a. | Fetal origin from ICA | 30% |

## Embryologic Considerations

The Circle of Willis forms during the 5th-7th weeks of embryonic development. Variations arise from:

- Differential regression of embryonic arterial channels
- Persistence of normally transient anastomoses
- Asymmetric development of paired structures
- Hemodynamic factors influencing vessel caliber

## Clinical Significance

### Collateral Function

The primary function of the Circle of Willis is to provide collateral blood flow when one of the major feeding arteries is occluded. An incomplete circle may:

- Limit compensation during carotid occlusion
- Increase stroke risk during carotid surgery
- Affect outcome after major vessel occlusion

### Aneurysm Associations

Variations in the Circle of Willis are associated with increased aneurysm risk:

- **Anterior communicating artery aneurysms** - Associated with A-1 segment hypoplasia
- **Junction aneurysms** - More common at points of asymmetric flow

### Surgical Considerations

Knowledge of Circle variations is essential for:

- Carotid endarterectomy planning
- Aneurysm clipping approaches
- Bypass surgery planning
- Endovascular procedure planning
