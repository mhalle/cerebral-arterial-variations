## Internal Carotid Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The internal carotid artery (ICA) ascends through the neck without branches, enters the skull through the carotid canal, traverses the cavernous sinus, and terminates by dividing into the anterior and middle cerebral arteries.

### Variations

#### Cervical Course
- **Sinuous course** - Pronounced curves or horseshoe shapes in the upper cervical portion
- **Complete loops** - May form in the upper cervical segment
- **Kinking** - May be due to cardiovascular disease rather than congenital variation

#### Branches in the Neck
The internal carotid rarely provides branches in the neck. However, reported variations include:
- Ascending pharyngeal artery
- Occipital artery
- Transverse facial artery
- Lingual artery
- Laryngeal artery
- Meningeal artery

#### Absence/Agenesis
- **Unilateral absence** - Rare but documented
- **Bilateral agenesis** - Extremely rare (0.07% in review of 1,407 angiograms)
- When absent, may be replaced by branches of maxillary artery
- Carotid canal(s) may be absent

#### Intracranial Variations
- May lie exposed within the middle ear cavity
- May pass through a bony ring formed by spicules from anterior and middle clinoid processes
- Aberrant vessel in cavernous sinus may anastomose with basilar artery

#### Relationship to External Carotid
In a study of 200 half-necks:
- Dorsolateral to external carotid: 38%
- Dorsal to external carotid: 38.5%
- Dorsomedial to external carotid: 23.5%

## Anterior Cerebral Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The anterior cerebral artery (ACA) arises from the ICA bifurcation, courses anteromedially above the optic chiasm, and is connected to its contralateral counterpart by the anterior communicating artery. The A-1 segment extends from its origin to the anterior communicating artery.

### Variations

#### Azygos (Unpaired) Anterior Cerebral Artery
- Both right and left ACAs may run as **one vessel** that divides distally
- One ACA may be a branch of the contralateral artery
- The two arteries may be **united into a single trunk** (like the basilar behind) that divides into right and left arteries
- **Incidence: ~4%**

#### Median/Third Anterior Cerebral Artery
- A third artery arising from the anterior communicating artery
- Courses in the longitudinal fissure giving branches to both hemispheres
- **Incidence: ~4.5%**

#### A-1 Segment Hypoplasia
- In 10% of 50 brains studied, A-1 segment diameter was ≤1.5 mm
- In only 1/50 was diameter <1.0 mm
- In study of 1,647 circles, A-1 segment hypoplastic in **7%**
- **Clinical significance**: Hypoplasia of A-1 segment may be associated with aneurysms of the anterior communicating artery

#### Size Asymmetry
- The two arteries frequently differ in size at their origin
- Larger artery may reinforce smaller through anterior communicating artery
- May develop so that both ACAs arise from one ICA via common bifurcating trunk

#### Duplication
- A-1 segment may be duplicated (infrequent)
- Arteries may be doubled

#### Other Variations
- Persistent olfactory artery may branch from ACA, coursing through cribriform plate to nasal cavity
- Anterior cerebrals may join to form median common anterior cerebral
- Recurrent artery of Heubner may be doubled or tripled

## Anterior Communicating Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The anterior communicating artery (AComm) connects the two anterior cerebral arteries, completing the anterior portion of the Circle of Willis.

### Variations

#### Number

| Pattern | Incidence |
|---------|-----------|
| Single vessel | ~81% (1,473/1,813 in one review) |
| Double (V or Y form) | 12.4% |
| Triple/plexiform | 3.09% |
| Absent | 0.22% (4/1,813) |
| Azygos ACA (small fusion areas) | 0.94% |

- **7% doubled** in one study of 50 brains
- When doubled or tripled, vessels may be similar or markedly different in diameter

#### Diameter
- In 50 brains: diameter ≤1.5 mm in **44%**, ≤1.0 mm in **16%**
- In 1,647 brains: so small as to restrict circulation in **6%** of cases

#### Length
- Usually 2-3 mm in length
- Range: 0.3-7 mm

#### Orientation
- Contrary to textbook descriptions, may not be strictly transverse
- Can be oriented obliquely or anterior-posteriorly
- Occurs when one ACA passes between hemispheres behind the other (80% of brains)

#### Shape
- May be curved, kinked, or tortuous rather than straight

### Clinical Significance
The anterior communicating artery is a common site for cerebral aneurysms, particularly in association with A-1 segment hypoplasia.

## Middle Cerebral Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The middle cerebral artery (MCA) is the largest branch of the ICA, coursing laterally into the Sylvian fissure to supply the lateral cerebral hemisphere.

### Variations

#### Initial Branching Pattern (from 300 autopsy study)

| Pattern | Incidence |
|---------|-----------|
| Bifurcation | 90% |
| Trifurcation | 10% |

#### Division Patterns (25 brains, 50 MCAs)
- Single vessel arising proximal to Sylvian fissure: 34%
- More than one major vessel proximal to Sylvian fissure: 26%
- Trifurcation at mouth of Sylvian fissure: 34%
- Bifurcation at mouth of Sylvian fissure: 16%

#### Terminal Branching (Lang, 1983)

| Pattern | Incidence |
|---------|-----------|
| Trifurcation | 53% |
| Quadrifurcation | 24% |
| Bifurcation | 20% |
| Quintafurcation | 3% |

#### Accessory Middle Cerebral Artery
- **Incidence: 3%**
- Unilateral in 8/9 cases, bilateral in 1/9
- Origin: Anterior cerebral artery (80%), Internal carotid (20%)
- Proceeds laterally in Sylvian fissure to share territory with MCA
- Important source of collateral blood supply to MCA territory

#### Other Variations
- May be absent or replaced by branch from deep cerebral artery
- May give rise to accessory posterior communicating artery
- Posterior communicating may connect to MCA instead of ICA trunk

#### Measurements
- Average length of 610 MCAs: 1.6 cm (range 0.5-3 cm)
- Diameter: 0.3-0.5 cm

#### Perforating Branches
- 6-20 small perforating branches from MCA
- 1-2 distal branches (Charcot's arteries) more prominent, up to 1.0 cm diameter
- Origin of distal striate arteries (600 MCAs):
  - From MCA trunk: 51.1%
  - At point of MCA division: 25.6%
  - From MCA branch: 20.3%

## Anterior Choroidal Artery

### Normal Anatomy
The anterior choroidal artery supplies the choroid plexus, optic tract, cerebral peduncle, and portions of the internal capsule.

### Variations

#### Origin

| Source | Incidence |
|--------|-----------|
| Internal carotid artery | 85% |
| Middle cerebral artery | 8% |
| Junction of ICA and MCA | 7% |

#### Other Variations
- May be diminished or absent
- When absent, replaced by branch of deep cerebral or posterior communicating artery

### References

## Recurrent Artery of Heubner

### Normal Anatomy
The recurrent artery of Heubner (medial striate artery) arises from the proximal ACA and courses back to enter the anterior perforated substance, supplying the head of the caudate nucleus, anterior limb of internal capsule, and anterior putamen.

### Variations

#### Origin
Descriptions of the origin vary among investigators:
- Typically arises from ACA in the region of the anterior communicating artery
- Courses caudally to enter anterior perforated substance medial to MCA perforators

#### Number
- **Most constant** of all branches at base of brain - present bilaterally in all cases in one study
- May be doubled bilaterally
- May be tripled

#### Unusual Origins
- Additional Heubner's artery may arise from accessory middle cerebral artery

### Clinical Significance
Given its supply to critical structures (internal capsule, basal ganglia), inadvertent injury can result in significant motor deficits.

### References
Heubner, O. (1872) Zur Topographie der Ernährungsgebiete der einzelnen Hirnarterien. Centralbl. Med. Wiss. 10:817-821.
